## Part A: Free University Courses Online

The world's leading universities offer their computer science courses online at no cost. Here are the best starting points.

**Harvard University -- CS50: Introduction to Computer Science**
The most widely recommended entry point. Covers computational thinking, programming fundamentals (C, Python, SQL, JavaScript), data structures, and web development. Entirely free on edX and on YouTube.
- edX: https://cs50.harvard.edu/x/
- YouTube (full course): https://www.youtube.com/cs50

**MIT OpenCourseWare (OCW)**
MIT publishes complete course materials -- lectures, problem sets, exams -- for hundreds of courses.
- 6.0001 Introduction to CS and Programming Using Python: https://ocw.mit.edu/courses/6-0001-introduction-to-computer-science-and-programming-in-python-fall-2016/
- Full CS catalog: https://ocw.mit.edu/search/?t=Computer%20Science

**Stanford Online**
Stanford offers several landmark courses free through various platforms.
- CS106A Programming Methodology (Java/Python): available on Stanford's YouTube channel
- CS106B Programming Abstractions (C++): available on Stanford's YouTube channel
- Machine Learning (Andrew Ng): https://www.coursera.org/learn/machine-learning

**Coursera (multiple universities)**
Many courses can be audited for free (full access to lectures and readings, no certificate).
- https://www.coursera.org/browse/computer-science
- Look for the "Audit" option on enrollment pages

**edX (multiple universities)**
Similar to Coursera -- free audit access to courses from Harvard, MIT, Berkeley, and others.
- https://www.edx.org/learn/computer-science

**freeCodeCamp**
A fully free, project-based platform covering web development, Python, data science, and more. Includes free certificates upon completion.
- https://www.freecodecamp.org/

**Khan Academy -- Computing**
Excellent for absolute beginners. Clear, visual explanations of how computers work, introductory programming, algorithms, and cryptography.
- https://www.khanacademy.org/computing

## Part B: Sample Graduate-Level Curriculum

Below is a structured path that mirrors a typical CS undergraduate-to-graduate progression. All materials are available free online. A learner can work through this over 12--18 months of steady study, or longer at a relaxed pace.

### Foundation (Months 1--3)
1. **Introduction to Computer Science** -- Harvard CS50 (broad overview, multiple languages)
2. **Programming Fundamentals** -- MIT 6.0001 (Python, problem-solving, computational thinking)
3. **Mathematics for CS** -- MIT 6.042J Mathematics for Computer Science (logic, proofs, discrete math, probability)

### Core (Months 4--8)
4. **Data Structures and Algorithms** -- Princeton Algorithms I & II on Coursera (Robert Sedgewick)
5. **Computer Architecture** -- Nand2Tetris (build a computer from logic gates up) -- https://www.nand2tetris.org/
6. **Operating Systems** -- available through MIT OCW or Georgia Tech via Udacity
7. **Databases** -- Stanford's "Databases" course (Jennifer Widom), available on edX/Stanford Online

### Intermediate (Months 9--12)
8. **Computer Networking** -- Stanford CS144, or the textbook companion site for Kurose/Ross
9. **Software Engineering** -- MIT 6.031 (software construction principles)
10. **Theory of Computation** -- MIT 18.404J (automata, computability, complexity)

### Advanced / Electives (Months 12--18)
11. **Artificial Intelligence** -- Berkeley CS188 (available on edX) or Stanford CS221
12. **Machine Learning** -- Stanford CS229 (Andrew Ng, full lectures on YouTube)
13. **Distributed Systems** -- MIT 6.824 (lectures and labs available online)
14. **Ethics and Social Impact of Computing** -- Harvard CS108 or Stanford CS182

### Capstone
The OSSU (Open Source Society University) curriculum on GitHub provides a well-maintained, community-reviewed sequence that maps to a full CS degree:
- https://github.com/ossu/computer-science

## Suggested Starting Path

For someone entirely new to technology and computer science, a gentle sequence might be:

1. **Week 1--2**: Khan Academy Computing -- get comfortable with the basic ideas
2. **Week 3--6**: Harvard CS50 on YouTube -- watch lectures, try the problem sets
3. **Week 7 onward**: Pick a programming language (Python is recommended) and work through freeCodeCamp or Corey Schafer's YouTube playlist, coding along with each lesson
4. **Ongoing**: Use GeeksforGeeks or Sanfoundry quizzes to test understanding after each topic
5. **When ready**: Follow the OSSU curriculum for a structured, complete path

The most important thing is not speed but consistency -- even 30 minutes of daily practice builds solid understanding over time.
